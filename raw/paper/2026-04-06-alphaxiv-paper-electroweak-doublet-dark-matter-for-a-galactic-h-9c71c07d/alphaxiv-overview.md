# Electroweak Doublet Dark Matter for a Galactic Halo Gamma-Ray Excess

- alphaXiv: https://www.alphaxiv.org/abs/2604.05016
- Source paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.05016

## The Search for Dark Matter and the Galactic Halo Excess

The identity of dark matter remains one of the most significant mysteries in modern physics. Among the various theoretical candidates, Weakly Interacting Massive Particles (WIMPs) are prominent because they can naturally explain the observed cosmic density of dark matter. This occurs through a process known as thermal freeze-out, where the dark matter particle’s mass and interaction strength—similar to those of the electroweak force—lead to the correct amount of material left over from the early universe. This coincidence is frequently termed the "WIMP miracle."

A common method to search for WIMPs is indirect detection, which looks for the remnants of dark matter annihilation in regions of high density, such as the center of our Galaxy. While the Galactic Center has long been a focus of these searches, recent analysis has identified a distinct signal known as the Galactic halo gamma-ray excess (GHGE). Unlike previous observations, this excess originates from a broader spatial region and features a higher photon-energy peak at approximately 20 GeV. When interpreted as a signature of dark matter, this signal points toward a dark matter particle with a mass $m_{\text{DM}} \sim 400\text{--}800\text{ GeV}$.

The research presented here investigates the minimal particle physics models capable of explaining this GHGE. By re-evaluating the gamma-ray spectrum, it was found that the signal is not only compatible with annihilation into bottom quarks ($b\bar{b}$) or $W$ bosons ($W^+ W^-$), but also with other heavy Standard Model particles such as top quarks ($t\bar{t}$), Higgs bosons ($hh$), and $Z$ bosons ($ZZ$). This flexibility suggests that the signal could arise from a variety of well-motivated extensions to the Standard Model, provided they satisfy stringent constraints from other experiments.

## Constraints and the WIMP Paradigm

Any model attempting to explain the GHGE must also be consistent with the broader landscape of dark matter physics. The thermal freeze-out mechanism predicts a specific annihilation cross-section, $\langle \sigma v \rangle \approx 3 \times 10^{-26} \text{ cm}^3/\text{s}$, to yield the correct relic abundance. However, the intensity of the GHGE signal suggests an annihilation rate in the present-day Galaxy that might be an order of magnitude higher than this thermal value. This discrepancy requires either a boost from astrophysical structures or a particle physics mechanism that enhances annihilation at low velocities.

Furthermore, dark matter models face severe pressure from direct detection experiments, which search for the recoil of atomic nuclei after a dark matter collision. Experiments like LZ and XENON have set very tight upper limits on the probability of these interactions. Models that couple dark matter directly to the Standard Model Higgs boson or the $Z$ boson often predict scattering rates that are already ruled out. Finally, any new particle must not have been seen in previous collider experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) or the Large Electron-Positron Collider (LEP).

## Evaluating Minimal Scalar Extensions

The authors systematically examined several simple extensions of the Standard Model to find a candidate that fits the GHGE data while avoiding these experimental pitfalls. One common model is Singlet Scalar Dark Matter, where a real scalar field $\phi$ is added to the Standard Model. This field is stabilized by a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry ($\phi \to -\phi$) and interacts with the Higgs field $H$ through a "portal" coupling:

$$ \mathcal{L} \supset -\frac{\lambda}{2} H^\dagger H \phi^2 $$

In this scenario, dark matter annihilates into Higgs and gauge bosons. To produce the correct relic abundance for a mass of several hundred GeV, the coupling $\lambda$ must be relatively large. Unfortunately, this same coupling leads to a high rate of elastic scattering with nuclei via Higgs exchange. The authors found that for the mass range of interest ($m_{\text{DM}} \sim 400\text{--}800\text{ GeV}$), this model predicts a direct detection signal much larger than the limits set by LZ and XENON, effectively ruling it out as an explanation for the GHGE.

Similarly, fermionic dark matter candidates like "Winos" or "Higgsinos" (which carry electroweak charges) were considered. While these are well-motivated in theories like supersymmetry, their thermal relic abundance is tied specifically to their gauge interactions. For these particles to constitute all of dark matter, their masses would need to be around 1 TeV for doublets or 3 TeV for triplets—values significantly higher than the 400–800 GeV range suggested by the GHGE.

## The Electroweak Doublet Model: A Consistent Solution

The research identifies the Inert Doublet Model (IDM) as the most promising candidate. In this model, an additional scalar field $\Phi$ is introduced with the same electroweak quantum numbers as the Standard Model Higgs field: $\Phi \sim (1, 2)_{1/2}$. Like the singlet model, it is stabilized by a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry ($\Phi \to -\Phi$), but unlike the Higgs, it does not acquire a vacuum expectation value.

The thermal relic abundance for an electroweak doublet is primarily determined by its interactions with the $W$ and $Z$ bosons. Remarkably, the mass required to match the observed cosmic relic density is naturally in the range of 500–600 GeV. This mass range is in excellent agreement with the values preferred by the GHGE data. When such a particle annihilates, it produces primarily longitudinal gauge bosons ($W^+_L W^-_L$ and $Z_L Z_L$) with a ratio of 2:1. The resulting gamma-ray spectrum provides a high-quality fit to the GHGE observations with a best-fit mass of approximately 460 GeV.

## Evading Constraints through Inelasticity and Enhancement

Despite the good fit for the mass and spectrum, the Electroweak Doublet model must overcome two hurdles: the direct detection limits and the required boost in annihilation cross-section.

### Inelasticity for Direct Detection
A standard electroweak doublet would normally interact with nuclei through the exchange of $Z$ bosons, a process that is extremely efficient and would have been detected long ago. To avoid this, the model utilizes the concept of "inelastic dark matter." By including a specific term in the scalar potential governed by a coupling $\lambda_5$, the neutral component of the doublet splits into two separate states with a small mass difference $\Delta m$. 

If the mass splitting $\Delta m$ is larger than the kinetic energy available in a typical dark matter-nucleus collision, the scattering process becomes kinematically forbidden. The condition to evade current limits is:

$$ \Delta m \gtrsim m_{\text{DM}} v^2 $$

For a mass of 500 GeV, this requires a splitting $\Delta m \gtrsim O(100\text{ keV})$. Interestingly, this splitting corresponds to a very small coupling $|\lambda_5| \sim 10^{-6}$, which is technically natural. Furthermore, the authors point out that this specific mass and splitting are consistent with certain recent anomalies reported in direct detection data, potentially linking two independent experimental signals.

### Sommerfeld Enhancement for the Gamma-Ray Signal
The annihilation cross-section needed to explain the GHGE is about ten times larger than the thermal freeze-out cross-section. To bridge this gap, the authors propose the inclusion of a light scalar field $\Sigma$. This field couples to the dark matter $\Phi$ via the interaction $\mathcal{L} \supset \mu \Sigma \Phi^\dagger \Phi$. The exchange of this light mediator creates an attractive Yukawa potential between dark matter particles.

At the slow speeds found in the Galactic halo ($v \approx 10^{-3}$), this potential focuses the wave functions of the colliding particles, a process known as Sommerfeld enhancement. This can increase the annihilation rate significantly in the present day without affecting the earlier freeze-out process in the hot early universe. To achieve the required factor of ten enhancement, the coupling parameter is found to be $\mu \sim 0.7 m_{\text{DM}}$.

This mechanism also helps the model stay consistent with observations of dwarf spheroidal galaxies. Dwarf galaxies have even lower velocity dispersions ($v \approx 5 \times 10^{-5}$) than the Milky Way. If the mediator $\Sigma$ has a mass $m_\Sigma \gg m_{\text{DM}} v_{\text{dwarf}} \sim 25\text{ MeV}$, the enhancement "saturates" and does not become excessively large in those systems. This allows the model to explain the GHGE in the Galactic halo while remaining below the detection limits in dwarf galaxies.

## Future Experimental Verification

The Electroweak Doublet model presented here is highly predictive and sits at the intersection of three different experimental frontiers.

1.  **Indirect Detection:** Future gamma-ray observations will continue to refine the GHGE spectrum. If the signal is indeed from dark matter, the precise shape should match the predicted 2:1 ratio of $W$ and $Z$ boson final states.
2.  **Direct Detection:** The inelastic nature of the dark matter suggests that experiments with higher energy thresholds or sensitivities to specific recoil patterns might see signals associated with the transition between the two neutral states. The parametric agreement with current direct detection anomalies provides a clear target for upcoming runs of experiments like LZ and XENONnT.
3.  **Collider Physics:** While current LHC data does not strongly constrain electroweak scalars in the 500 GeV range, future lepton colliders (such as a Muon Collider or an International Linear Collider) would be ideal for testing this model. These machines can produce electroweak states cleanly and would be able to definitively discover or rule out the existence of a dark matter doublet at the 500 GeV scale.

By combining the principles of minimality and consistency with a wide array of data, the authors demonstrate that an electroweak doublet remains a viable and compelling explanation for the observed signatures of dark matter in our Galaxy.
