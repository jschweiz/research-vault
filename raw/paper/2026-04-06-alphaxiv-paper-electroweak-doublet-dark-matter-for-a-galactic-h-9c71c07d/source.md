# Electroweak Doublet Dark Matter for a Galactic Halo Gamma-Ray Excess

## alphaXiv Summary

An electroweak doublet scalar dark matter model is proposed to explain the Galactic halo gamma-ray excess (GHGE), naturally yielding a dark matter mass around 500-600 GeV and dominant annihilation into W and Z bosons. This framework incorporates inelastic dark matter with a mass splitting consistent with a direct-detection anomaly and uses Sommerfeld enhancement to match the observed annihilation rate while avoiding dwarf galaxy constraints.

## Abstract

Weakly interacting massive particles provide a well-motivated framework for dark matter, naturally reproducing the observed relic abundance through thermal freeze-out. A recent claim of an indirect-detection signal from the Galactic halo, consistent with dark matter annihilation in the mass range 400--800 GeV, motivates a reexamination of minimal models that can account for such a signal while remaining consistent with existing constraints. In this paper, we analyze the simplest extensions of the Standard Model capable of explaining the signal. We show that electroweak doublet dark matter with Higgs-portal interactions provides a natural and economical explanation. The model predicts annihilation predominantly into longitudinal gauge bosons with characteristic branching fractions and allows for inelastic dark matter with a mass splitting of order 100 keV, intriguingly consistent with a recent direct-detection anomaly. Possible enhancements of the present-day annihilation rate relative to the thermal value are also discussed, including a simple extension with a light scalar field, whose mass can be chosen such that the enhancement is suppressed in dwarf galaxies.

## Problem

- A recently reported Galactic halo gamma-ray excess (GHGE) requires a dark matter (DM) particle physics explanation, suggesting a DM mass of 400-800 GeV and an annihilation cross section potentially exceeding the canonical thermal value.
- Many minimal WIMP models face challenges in simultaneously satisfying constraints from thermal relic abundance, direct detection, collider searches, and other indirect detection limits.
- Reconciling a potentially high present-day DM annihilation rate in the Galactic halo with stringent upper limits from dwarf spheroidal galaxies poses a significant challenge for indirect detection models.

## Method

- The authors systematically examine minimal Standard Model extensions, including electroweakly charged fermions and Higgs-portal scalars, to identify a dark matter candidate for the GHGE.
- They propose an electroweak doublet scalar field (Φ) with a Z2 symmetry, where its lightest neutral component serves as the dark matter, whose thermal relic abundance naturally places its mass in the range suggested by the GHGE.
- The model incorporates inelastic dark matter through a small mass splitting (Δm) to evade direct detection constraints and utilizes a light scalar mediator (Σ) to induce Sommerfeld enhancement for the present-day annihilation cross section.

## Results

- Electroweak doublet scalar dark matter naturally explains the GHGE, with a best-fit mass of approximately 460 GeV and dominant annihilation into longitudinal W and Z bosons, yielding a cross section of about 8 × 10^-25 cm^3/s.
- The required inelastic mass splitting of O(100 keV) effectively suppresses elastic direct detection, allowing the model to satisfy current direct detection bounds and align with a recently reported direct-detection excess.
- Sommerfeld enhancement, mediated by a light scalar (mΣ ≲ 500 MeV), boosts the Galactic halo annihilation rate to match the GHGE, while its suppression in dwarf galaxies (for mΣ ≫ 25 MeV) ensures consistency with their stricter indirect detection limits.

## Takeaways

- The GHGE signal is not uniquely explained by b¯b or W+W- final states; other heavy Standard Model final states such as t¯t, hh, and ZZ also provide comparable fits, broadening the scope for viable dark matter models.
- Electroweak doublet dark matter provides an economical explanation for the GHGE, with its thermal relic abundance naturally fixing the dark matter mass around 500-600 GeV, consistent with the observed signal.
- A small, technically natural mass splitting of O(100 keV) for inelastic dark matter not only resolves direct-detection conflicts but also parametrically aligns with a reported direct-detection anomaly, suggesting a potential multi-channel connection.
