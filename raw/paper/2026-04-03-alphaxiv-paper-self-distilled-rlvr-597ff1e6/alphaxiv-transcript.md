This new work, coming from researchers at the Chinese Academy of Sciences and JD.COM, offers a critical analysis of that trend and proposes a new path forward. It really gets at the core challenge of how to give models fine-grained feedback without causing unintended side effects.

**Noah:** Hi Professor. You mentioned the 'Self-Distilled Reasoner' paper. My understanding was that on-policy self-distillation, or OPSD, was quite effective. Is this paper suggesting there's a problem with it?
**John:** Exactly. The authors start by showing that while OPSD looks good on paper, it has a fundamental flaw. The core problem in training reasoning models is credit assignment. Standard methods, like Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards, or RLVR, give a single reward to the entire generated answer. It’s like telling a basketball team 'you won' but not telling them which players made the key shots. Every token in the reasoning chain gets the same credit, which is inefficient.
**Noah:** Okay, so OPSD was meant to solve that by having a 'teacher' version of the model, which has access to the correct answer, provide token-by-token guidance to the 'student' version.
**John:** Correct. It provides dense, per-token supervision. But the authors found this leads to two major issues: training instability and 'privileged information leakage.' Empirically, they saw that models trained with OPSD would improve for a while and then suddenly get worse. When they looked closer, the models were learning to cheat by referencing cues from the privileged information—the ground-truth answer—that they would never have access to during a real test. The paper's first major contribution is a formal proof explaining why this happens. They identify what they call an 'irreducible mutual information gap' between the teacher and student, which makes the training objective fundamentally ill-posed and forces the model to encode these spurious correlations.
**Noah:** So the objective itself is flawed. How do they fix it?
**John:** That's their proposed method: RLSD, or RLVR with Self-Distillation. Instead of trying to make the student's output distribution match the privileged teacher's, they decouple their roles. The environment reward—the simple 'correct' or 'incorrect' signal from RLVR—still determines the direction of the update. It's the only thing that tells the model whether to reinforce or penalize a sequence. The self-distillation signal is repurposed entirely. It's now used only to modulate the magnitude of that update on a per-token basis.
**Noah:** Can you walk through how that works? How does the model determine the magnitude for each token?
**John:** Certainly. The process has three main steps. First, for each token the student generates, they calculate a 'Privileged Information Gain.' This is essentially the log-probability of that token under the teacher's context, which includes the ground-truth answer, minus the log-probability under the student's normal context. This value tells you how much more likely that specific token becomes when you know the final answer. It quantifies how much 'evidence' the token provides for the correct solution.
**Noah:** And this gain is calculated with a stop-gradient, right? So it's just a raw number, not another loss term.
**John:** Precisely. It's just a weight. The second step is 'Direction-Aware Reweighting.' They take this gain value and combine it with the sign of the overall sequence reward. If the entire answer was correct, tokens with high gain get a much larger weight, concentrating the reward on the key reasoning steps. If the answer was incorrect, tokens that were strongly disfavored by the privileged information—meaning they were likely part of the error—get a larger weight, concentrating the blame on the specific mistake. The environment's reward signal maintains total control over the direction—positive or negative—while the distillation signal just points to where the credit or blame should go.
**Noah:** Wait, what about the third step? You mentioned clipping.
**John:** Yes, to prevent instability from excessively large weights, they clip the per-token advantage within a certain range. This acts as a trust region, preventing the teacher's influence from overpowering the learning signal. The end result is a token-level advantage that replaces the uniform advantage in the standard RLVR algorithm. It’s a drop-in enhancement.
**Noah:** So the big shift is that the teacher is no longer a generative target. It’s an evaluator that assigns importance scores. The paper mentioned an 'Impossibility Trilemma' for OPSD. Does this new approach resolve it?
**John:** It does. The trilemma they identify for distribution-matching OPSD is that you cannot simultaneously have objective stability, sustained improvement, and be free from information leakage. RLSD breaks this trilemma because it's no longer a distribution-matching problem. By isolating the privileged information to only influence the update magnitude, not its direction, they prove it's structurally immune to leakage. The results show it achieves a higher performance ceiling than standard RLVR and avoids the performance collapse seen in OPSD.
**Noah:** This seems related to other work on 'teacher hacking,' where a student model overfits to a teacher's flaws. But here, the problem is more fundamental—the student is hacking the privileged information itself.
**John:** An excellent connection. The mechanism is similar, but the source of the problem is different. 'Teacher hacking' often assumes a fixed, imperfect teacher. Here, the teacher is the model itself, just with extra information. The paper's diagnosis of the mutual information gap provides a more formal, theoretical reason for why this specific form of self-distillation is problematic, which is a significant contribution that could inform the design of future distillation methods.
**John:** So, to wrap up, this work provides a sharp diagnosis of why a popular self-distillation technique fails and offers an elegant, effective solution. The key takeaway is the decoupling of roles: use the reliable, sparse environmental reward to set the update direction, and use the dense, privileged self-distillation signal to modulate the update's magnitude for fine-grained credit assignment. This simple change leads to more stable training and better reasoning performance with minimal computational overhead. Thanks for listening.
