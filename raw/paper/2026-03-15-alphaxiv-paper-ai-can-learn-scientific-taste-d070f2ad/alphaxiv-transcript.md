The core motivation is that current LLMs can generate many novel ideas, but they struggle to distinguish between a genuinely impactful concept and one that's just superficially new.
**Noah:** But 'impact' is so subjective and can take years to measure. How do they quantify that for training a model?
**John:** They use a proxy: citation counts. The underlying assumption is that, at a large scale, citations reflect the collective judgment or 'community verdict' on a paper's influence. To address obvious biases, like older papers having more citations or different citation norms across fields, they construct a dataset called SciJudgeBench. It consists of nearly 700,000 pairs of papers from arXiv. Each pair is matched by field and publication date, and the preference label is simply which paper received significantly more citations. This pairwise approach allows the model to learn relative importance rather than predicting an absolute, and often noisy, citation number.
**Noah:** So they're framing it as a preference learning problem. That sounds like RLHF.
**John:** Exactly, but with a key twist. Instead of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, they call their method Reinforcement Learning from Community Feedback, or RLCF. This model acts as a reward model. Given two abstracts, it doesn't just pick one; it generates a reasoning trace explaining its choice before making a judgment. This is trained using an algorithm called Group Relative Policy Optimization, or GRPO, where the reward is simply whether its prediction matches the citation-based label in the dataset.
**Noah:** Okay, so Scientific Judge learns to predict community preference. How does that help with generating new ideas?
**John:** That's the third stage: preference alignment. They train another model, 'Scientific Thinker,' to generate novel research ideas based on a seed paper. Since there's no ground truth for what a 'good idea' is, they use the Scientific Judge as the reward signal. For a given seed, the Thinker generates a batch of new ideas. The Judge then performs a round-robin tournament, comparing all the ideas against each other. An idea's reward is its win rate in this tournament. By optimizing for this reward, the Thinker learns to generate ideas that the Judge, and by extension the scientific community, would likely find impactful.
