# TriAttention: Efficient Long Reasoning with Trigonometric KV Compression

- alphaXiv: https://www.alphaxiv.org/abs/2604.04921
- Source paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.04921

The development of Large Language Models (LLMs) has increasingly shifted toward "long-reasoning" capabilities. Models are now expected to produce extensive Chain-of-Thought (CoT) sequences that can span tens of thousands of tokens to solve complex mathematical or coding problems. However, this progress is hindered by a critical computational bottleneck: the Key-Value (KV) cache. Every token generated by a Transformer-based model requires storing its intermediate representations (Keys and Values) to avoid redundant calculations in future steps. As the sequence length grows, the memory required for this cache scales linearly, eventually exceeding the available GPU memory and slowing down inference significantly.

*Figure 1: Comparison of TriAttention against Full Attention and baseline compression methods. (A) Shows throughput improvements, reaching 2.5x speedup at comparable accuracy. (B) Illustrates KV memory reduction, where TriAttention uses 10.7x less memory while maintaining the performance of the full model.*

To manage this, researchers have developed various KV cache compression techniques that prune "unimportant" tokens. However, most existing methods struggle with stability during long reasoning tasks. TriAttention addresses these limitations by identifying a fundamental, model-intrinsic property in the representations of LLMs—specifically, the concentration of vectors before they are modified by positional encodings. This insight allows for a more reliable estimation of which tokens will be important for future reasoning steps.

## The Challenge of Long Context and RoPE

In a standard Transformer, the attention mechanism determines the relevance of past tokens to the current query by calculating the dot product between Query $Q$ and Key $K$ vectors (often represented as $q \cdot k^T$). To help the model understand the order of words, modern LLMs use Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE). RoPE works by rotating the $Q$ and $K$ vectors by an angle that depends on their position in the sequence. 

While effective for training, RoPE creates a significant problem for KV cache compression. Because the vectors are constantly rotating as the sequence progresses, a query at position 10,000 sees a key at position 100 through a very different "angular lens" than a query at position 200 did. This makes the observation window for estimating token importance very narrow. Most current compression methods look at "post-RoPE" representations—the vectors after they have been rotated. Because of this rotation, only very recent queries provide useful information about a key's importance, leading models to accidentally evict keys that might be critical for a reasoning step that occurs thousands of tokens later.

## Identifying Pre-RoPE Concentration

The core contribution of this work is the discovery of "Q/K Concentration" in the *pre-RoPE* space. By analyzing models like Qwen and Llama, the authors found that before the positional rotation is applied, the $Q$ and $K$ vectors are not scattered randomly. Instead, they are highly concentrated around fixed, non-zero centers.

![Q/K Concentration Analysis](https://paper-assets.alphaxiv.org/figures-normalized/figures/2604.04921v1/x1.png)
*Figure 2: Analysis of vector distributions. (A) Pre-RoPE vectors show high concentration around specific centers. (B) Post-RoPE vectors are scattered across a circle due to positional rotation. (C) Quantitative distribution of concentration $R$ across different attention heads. (D) Comparison showing how predicted attention matches the ground truth.*

This concentration is remarkably stable across different positions and input contexts. To quantify this, the researchers used the Mean Resultant Length (represented as $R$), where $R \approx 1.0$ signifies perfect concentration. They observed that in most attention heads, $R > 0.95$. This stability implies that the "average" query or key for a given attention head is a good representation of any specific query or key in that head. 

## Predicting Attention with Trigonometric Series

When $Q$ and $K$ vectors are highly concentrated, the complex math of the attention mechanism simplifies. By substituting the "center" vectors (the means found during a one-time offline calibration) into the RoPE attention formula, the interaction between a query and a key can be approximated by a trigonometric series. 

This series depends primarily on the distance between the query and the key, $\Delta = p_q - p_k$. This allows the model to predict a "distance preference" for each attention head. Some heads might naturally prefer looking at tokens 500 positions back, while others prefer 5,000. Because this preference is derived from stable pre-RoPE centers, it remains reliable even over very long sequences where post-RoPE methods become unstable. The authors found that this trigonometric reconstruction is highly accurate, with a mean Pearson correlation $\bar{r} > 0.5$ across a majority of attention heads in various models.

## The TriAttention Framework

TriAttention uses these insights to score the importance of each key in the cache. The framework operates in two main stages: offline calibration and online pruning.

### 1. Offline Calibration
Before the model is deployed, it is run on a small set of representative data (around 10,000 tokens) to calculate the mean vectors and norms for $Q$ and $K$ in every attention head. These statistics are "model-intrinsic"—they don't change much regardless of whether the model is solving a math problem or writing code.

### 2. Scoring Mechanism
When the model is generating text and the KV cache exceeds a certain budget, TriAttention assigns an importance score to each key $k$. This score has two components:

*   **Trigonometric Series Score ($S_{\text{trig}}$):** This component uses the pre-RoPE center of the queries to predict how much attention the key $k$ will receive from future queries at various distances $\Delta$.
$$
S_{\text{trig}}(k, \Delta) = \sum_{f} \|E[q_f]\| \cdot \|k_f\| \cdot \cos(\omega_f \Delta + \phi_f)
$$
*   **Norm-Based Score ($S_{\text{norm}}$):** This accounts for the magnitude of the key vector, weighted by how concentrated the head is ($1 - R_f$). This captures importance that isn't purely distance-dependent.
$$
S_{\text{norm}}(k) = \sum_{f} (1 - R_f) \cdot E[\|q_f\|] \cdot \|k_f\|
$$

The final importance score $\tilde{S}(k)$ for a key is the average of these scores across a set of diverse future distances $D = \{1, 2, 4, \dots, 2^{16}\}$. This ensures the model retains keys that are useful for both immediate and long-term reasoning.

$$
\tilde{S}(k) = \frac{1}{|D|} \sum_{\Delta \in D} (S_{\text{trig}}(k, \Delta) + S_{\text{norm}}(k))
$$

### 3. Periodic Pruning
Instead of pruning at every single step, which would be computationally expensive, the system performs pruning periodically (e.g., every $\beta = 128$ tokens). It calculates the scores, keeps the top $B$ tokens according to the budget, and discards the rest.

## Experimental Performance

The effectiveness of TriAttention was tested on challenging mathematical reasoning benchmarks like AIME24, AIME25, and MATH500. These tasks are particularly difficult for compressed models because losing a single intermediate reasoning step can lead to a completely wrong final answer.

![Accuracy vs Budget Benchmarks](https://paper-assets.alphaxiv.org/figures-normalized/figures/2604.04921v1/fig_kv_budget_accuracy_combined_4panel.png)
*Figure 4: Reasoning accuracy across different KV cache budgets. (A, B, C) Show that TriAttention consistently outperforms baseline compression (R-KV) and often matches Full Attention even with significantly reduced budgets. (D) Demonstrates superior performance in maintaining memory during deep recursive reasoning.*

In these tests, TriAttention consistently outperformed existing baselines. For example, on the AIME25 benchmark using a Qwen3-8B model, TriAttention achieved 32.9% accuracy, nearly doubling the performance of the R-KV baseline (17.5%) at the same budget. In many cases, TriAttention was able to match the accuracy of the full, uncompressed model while using 10 times less KV memory.

### Retention of Critical Information
A unique "Recursive Simulation" benchmark was used to test if the model "forgets" intermediate steps. In this task, the model must maintain a state through multiple levels of recursion. As the depth of reasoning increases, traditional compression methods often suffer from "catastrophic forgetting" where a missing key breaks the entire chain. TriAttention remained stable even at high recursion depths, indicating it correctly identifies and preserves the essential "anchors" of a reasoning sequence.

## Real-World Application and Efficiency

Beyond accuracy, the primary goal of KV cache compression is to make LLMs faster and more accessible. By reducing the memory footprint, TriAttention allows for higher throughput (more tokens generated per second) and enables larger models to run on consumer-grade hardware.

On the MATH500 benchmark, TriAttention provided a 6.3x throughput gain compared to full attention. This means a model that previously generated 223 tokens per second could now generate over 1,400 tokens per second.

The authors demonstrated this practical benefit with **OpenClaw**, a multi-turn AI agent. Without compression, running a 32B-parameter model on a single RTX 4090 GPU quickly leads to "Out-of-Memory" (OOM) errors during long conversations. With TriAttention enabled, the same model was able to process multiple project files and generate a comprehensive weekly report without crashing, successfully managing the limited memory available on a single consumer card.

## Conclusion

TriAttention represents a shift in how we think about KV cache compression. Instead of relying on noisy, position-dependent attention scores from the current window, it leverages the hidden stability of pre-RoPE representations. By recognizing that attention heads have intrinsic "distance preferences" that can be modeled as trigonometric series, it provides a robust way to prune the KV cache without breaking the fragile chains of logic required for long reasoning. This approach not only improves the accuracy of long-context models but also significantly lowers the hardware barriers for deploying powerful LLMs in real-world, long-form applications.

## Relevant Citations

- Roformer: Enhanced transformer with rotary position embedding: This paper introduces Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE), which is a foundational concept for the TriAttention paper. TriAttention's core thesis is built upon analyzing the properties of query and key vectors in the pre-RoPE space, making this citation essential for understanding the underlying mechanism.
- R-KV: Redundancy-aware KV cache compression for reasoning models: This work is the primary baseline and competitor used for comparison throughout the experiments in the TriAttention paper. The authors consistently benchmark TriAttention against R-KV to demonstrate its superior performance in reasoning tasks and efficiency, making it the most critical point of comparison.
- H2o: Heavy-hitter oracle for efficient generative inference of large language models: This paper is a prominent example of the attention-based KV cache compression methods that TriAttention critiques. The main paper argues that such methods, which estimate key importance from recent post-RoPE attention scores, are unstable, and H2O is cited as a key example of this approach.
- Efficient streaming language models with attention sinks: This citation introduces StreamingLLM and the concept of 'attention sinks,' a well-known heuristic for managing KV cache in long-context scenarios. The TriAttention paper references this work as an early, rule-based method that its more adaptive, content-aware approach aims to improve upon.
