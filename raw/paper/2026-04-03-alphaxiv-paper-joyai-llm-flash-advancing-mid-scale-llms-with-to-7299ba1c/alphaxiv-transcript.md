**John:** It is, but PPO and its variants can struggle with what the authors call multi-scale instability. At a high level, FiberPO decomposes the policy update into two distinct parts: a global component and a local component. Imagine you're trying to guide a large group of hikers—that's the policy. The global component is like setting the general direction for the entire group for the day, ensuring they don't wander completely off the mountain. This is the trajectory-level control.

**John:** Exactly. Then you have the local component. This is like giving each individual hiker a small amount of leeway to choose their specific footing on the path. This is the token-level control. This means a globally 'good' trajectory doesn't automatically cause every single token within it to get a large, potentially noisy gradient update. And conversely, a single 'bad' token doesn't get completely ignored just because the overall trajectory was acceptable.
