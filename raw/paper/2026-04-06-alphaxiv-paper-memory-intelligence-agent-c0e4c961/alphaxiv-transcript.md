It does this through a tripartite architecture: a Memory Manager, a Planner, and an Executor. The Manager handles a compressed, non-parametric memory of past search trajectories. The Planner, a smaller parametric model, learns from these experiences to generate high-level strategies. The Executor then takes that plan and interacts with external tools to find an answer.

This internalizes the experience, making the Planner itself a form of parametric memory. This avoids the need to constantly feed massive context into the final execution step.

**John:** That's a key part of their methodology. Training them jointly is difficult because the feedback loop is indirect. In stage two, they freeze the Executor and train the Planner to generate better plans, with rewards based on the final answer and the effectiveness of its reflection and replanning when the Executor gets stuck. This signal is strong enough to guide both the selection of memories for storage and the continuous, online fine-tuning of the Planner agent, allowing MIA to evolve and improve on the fly.
